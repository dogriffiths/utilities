Vardy v Rooney

[2020] EWHC 3156 (QB) - High Court Summary

The Case Overview

What this libel case was about

  • Claimant: Rebekah Vardy (wife of Jamie Vardy)
  • Defendant: Coleen Rooney (wife of Wayne Rooney)
  • Issue: Trial of meaning as preliminary issue in libel claim
  • Background: Rooney accused Vardy's Instagram account of leaking private stories to The Sun
  • Date: November 2020 judgment by Mr Justice Warby

The Infamous Post

What Coleen Rooney actually wrote

On 9 October 2019, Rooney posted on Instagram and Twitter describing how she had:

  • Suspected a leak: Someone was informing The Sun of her private posts
  • Set a trap: Blocked everyone except one account from viewing her stories
  • Posted false stories: About gender selection, TV return, basement flooding
  • Caught the culprit: Stories appeared in The Sun newspaper
"It's ……………. Rebekah Vardy's account"

The Legal Battle

What each side claimed the post meant

Vardy's interpretation (Chase Level 1):

The post accused her of "consistently and repeatedly" betraying Rooney's trust by leaking private information to The Sun.

Rooney's interpretation (Chase Level 2):

The post only suggested there were "reasonable grounds to suspect" Vardy was responsible.

Key Legal Principles

How courts determine meaning in defamation

  • Ordinary reasonable reader test: What would a typical reader understand?
  • No elaborate analysis: Courts avoid over-thinking social media posts
  • Read as a whole: Context matters, including the full publication
  • Author's intent irrelevant: Only the meaning conveyed matters
  • Social media speed: Twitter/Instagram readers scan quickly and impressionistically

The Court's Decision

Judge ruled in favor of Vardy's interpretation

The court found the post bore a Chase Level 1 meaning - that Vardy was guilty of the wrongdoing, not merely suspected.

"Over a period of years Ms Vardy had regularly and frequently abused her status as a trusted follower... by secretly informing The Sun newspaper"
  • Structured narrative: Post told a story building to a revelation
  • "I know for certain": Rooney claimed definitive proof
  • Clear identification: Vardy was specifically named as the wrongdoer

The "Account" Defense

Why Rooney's argument failed

Rooney argued using "Rebekah Vardy's account" suggested uncertainty about who actually operated it.

  • Judge disagreed: Ordinary readers wouldn't focus on this technicality
  • No common knowledge: Can't assume readers know celebrities use PR teams
  • Context matters: Nothing else suggested someone else was responsible
  • Consistent wrongdoing: Alleged behavior over years pointed to account holder

Social Media Implications

What this means for defamation law

  • Careful wording matters: Even social media posts can be legally significant
  • Context is key: Courts look at the whole narrative, not isolated words
  • Celebrity assumptions: Can't rely on "common knowledge" about PR teams
  • Technical distinctions fail: Ordinary readers don't parse legal technicalities
  • Serious allegations: Even on social media, accusations can have real legal consequences

The Bigger Picture

Why this case mattered

This was just the preliminary "meaning" trial. The case would continue to determine whether the allegations were actually true and what damages (if any) should be awarded.

  • High-profile precedent: Celebrity social media disputes in court
  • Public interest: Massive media coverage of "Wagatha Christie" case
  • Legal clarity: How courts interpret social media accusations
  • Defamation evolution: Traditional libel law meeting modern platforms
